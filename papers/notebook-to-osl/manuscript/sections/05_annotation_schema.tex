\section{Annotation schema}
\label{sec:schema}

The annotation schema is the central artefact of the method. It defines what must be preserved when a raw note is transformed into an operator-strategy fragment. The schema is not intended to be universal. It is a first version grounded in four literature streams: contextual inquiry, cognitive task analysis, requirements engineering, and MBSE/semantic Digital Twin modelling.

\subsection{Schema design rationale}

The schema is designed to prevent three common failures. First, raw field notes can become disconnected from their source context. Second, operator statements can be simplified into brittle if--then rules that ignore uncertainty and exceptions. Third, qualitative insights can remain too informal to support later engineering work. Table~\ref{tab:schema-rationale} shows how the main schema groups respond to these failures.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering

\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{p{0.25\textwidth}p{0.35\textwidth}X}
\toprule
Schema group & Literature basis & Purpose in Digital Twin development \\
\midrule

\subsection{Status model}

Each note and fragment should have a status. The status prevents the repository from mixing raw, interpreted, validated, rejected, sensitive, and formalised material. Table~\ref{tab:status-model} lists the proposed status values.

Status & Meaning \\
\midrule

\texttt{selected} & Chosen for annotation because it appears relevant to operator strategy. \\

\subsection{Validation protocol}

Validation operationalises the interpretive check required by contextual inquiry and the source-trace discipline required by requirements engineering. For each annotated fragment, the operator or domain expert should be asked:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Is this interpretation of the note correct?
    \item What important context or condition is missing?
    \item Would this cue mean the same thing for another material, tool state, operation, or machine condition?
    \item What would a novice likely misunderstand here?
    \item When would this action be inappropriate, unsafe, unnecessary, or too late?
    \item What exception or alternative explanation should be recorded?
    \item Who is actually responsible for acting, validating, or escalating?
    \item Could this fragment be useful in a Digital Twin as an alert, explanation, validation gate, or recommendation?
    \item Should any part of this note be treated as sensitive and excluded from publication or tooling?
    \item What confidence should be assigned to the fragment as written?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why uncertainty is a first-class field}

The schema treats uncertainty as a first-class field because operator strategies are often conditional. For example, a sound may indicate tool wear in one material but be normal in another. A vibration pattern may be acceptable during roughing but problematic during finishing. A Digital Twin that ignores these distinctions can create false alarms or misleading recommendations. Preserving uncertainty also supports later validation because it makes clear which parts of a fragment are confirmed and which parts remain interpretive.
