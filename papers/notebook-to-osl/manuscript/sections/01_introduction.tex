\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Digital Twins are increasingly presented as a foundation for monitoring, simulation, prediction, optimisation, and decision support in manufacturing. Much of the literature emphasises the integration of physical assets, digital models, data flows, and lifecycle information \cite{grieves2017digital,fuller2020digital,negri2017review,semeraro2021digital}. These perspectives are necessary, but they do not fully explain how a Digital Twin should become useful to the people who operate, interpret, and intervene in the manufacturing process. In shop-floor settings, relevant knowledge is often practical, situated, and experience-based. Operators may recognise abnormality through sound, vibration, rhythm, surface finish, tool behaviour, or small deviations from expected machine response. Such knowledge may be central to Digital Twin development, but it rarely appears directly in formal models.

This paper focuses on a specific early-stage problem: how can operator strategies be captured when they are learned through practical training rather than stated as formal requirements? We use the term \emph{operator strategy} to refer to a situated pattern that relates a perceived cue, operating context, interpretation, possible action, rationale, uncertainty, and responsibility. A strategy is therefore more than a single observation and less than a complete formal model. It is an intermediate knowledge object that can later inform Digital Twin requirements, decision-support rules, dashboards, alerts, simulation scenarios, or formal modelling constructs.

Existing elicitation approaches such as interviews, workshops, and document analysis can provide valuable information. However, they can also detach operator knowledge from the context in which it is normally used. A workshop may ask an operator what they do when tool wear occurs. A situated training session may instead reveal that the operator first notices a change in sound, checks whether the material or operation makes that sound plausible, watches for persistence, and only then decides whether to stop, inspect, or continue. The difference matters because a Digital Twin that only models the final action may fail to represent the reasoning that makes the action trustworthy.

We propose a method called \emph{Notebook-to-OSL}. The method is designed for situations where a researcher or engineer stands beside an operator and is explicitly trained in the work process. During training, the researcher takes short notes on a laptop. The notes are not forced into a rigid schema during the session because premature structuring can interrupt the training and distort what is noticed. After the session, the notes are reviewed in a lightweight application or annotation workflow. Each relevant note is selected, annotated, validated, and transformed into a candidate fragment for an Operator Strategy Language (OSL). OSL is treated here as a target representation, not as a completed language specification. The purpose of this paper is to describe the elicitation and transformation step that precedes formal language design.

The paper addresses the following research questions:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.4cm,style=nextline]
    \item[RQ1] How can situated operator training be used to elicit operator strategies for Digital Twin development?
    \item[RQ2] How can raw laptop notes from CNC operator training be transformed into structured OSL candidate fragments?
    \item[RQ3] What annotation categories are needed to preserve cues, actions, rationale, uncertainty, context, responsibility, and validation needs?
\end{description}

The paper makes four contributions. First, it frames apprenticeship-like operator training as a legitimate elicitation setting for Digital Twin development. Second, it proposes a workflow from raw training note to annotated operator-strategy fragment. Third, it introduces an annotation schema for preserving operational cues, interpretations, actions, uncertainty, exceptions, responsibility, and validation needs. Fourth, it illustrates the method through a CNC manufacturing example. The goal is not to replace interviews, workshops, machine data, or formal modelling, but to provide an intermediate layer between situated operator practice and formal Digital Twin artefacts.
